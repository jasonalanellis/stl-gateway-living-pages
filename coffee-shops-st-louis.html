Best Coffee Shops in St. Louis

Updated May 2026 — STL Gateway Living

St. Louis's coffee scene has matured significantly over the past decade, moving well past the chains to develop a roster of independent roasters and cafes that rival those of larger coastal cities. The city's neighborhoods — each with its own character — have shaped distinct coffee cultures. Here is the definitive guide to where locals actually go.

Quick Reference: Top Picks by Category

  • Best espresso: Sump Coffee (Benton Park)
  • Best for work: Blueprint Coffee (The Grove)
  • Best neighborhood feel: Kaldi's Coffee (DeMun/Clayton)
  • Best late-night: Meshuggah Cafe (University City)
  • Best atmosphere: Comet Coffee (Cherokee Street)
  • Best South City: Foundation Grounds (Dutchtown)
  • Best for pour-over: Water Street Coffee (Maplewood)
  • Best third-wave roastery: Coma Coffee (downtown)

The Essential St. Louis Coffee Shops

Benton Park

Sump Coffee

If St. Louis has one coffee shop that would survive a national comparison on pure merit, it is Sump. Founded by Scott Carey with a rigorous approach to sourcing, roasting, and extraction, Sump has become a pilgrimage destination for serious coffee drinkers. The espresso is exceptional — consistently one of the best in the Midwest. The space is small and industrial, with exposed brick and the shop's own roaster visible from the counter. There is no ambient music, which some find austere and others find refreshing. The baristas know coffee the way sommeliers know wine.

Sump roasts on-site and sells its beans retail. If you are buying beans to take home from a St. Louis trip, Sump is the correct answer.

The Grove / Multiple Locations

Blueprint Coffee

Blueprint occupies a converted mid-century building in The Grove (Forest Park Southeast), with high ceilings, generous natural light, and a serious commitment to craft. The espresso program is strong; the pour-over bar is executed with care. Blueprint has expanded to a few additional locations, but the original Grove shop remains the flagship. The space is large enough to accommodate laptop workers without making the experience feel like a coworking space, which is a difficult balance to strike.

Blueprint's baristas are consistently well-trained, and the shop has been central to the development of St. Louis's third-wave coffee culture since its founding in 2013.

DeMun / Clayton / Multiple Locations

Kaldi's Coffee

Kaldi's is the hometown brand that St. Louis embraced before specialty coffee became a national trend. Founded in 1994 in the DeMun neighborhood of Clayton, Kaldi's grew slowly and deliberately into a multi-location operation that maintains quality across its cafes. The DeMun location, on a tree-lined street with sidewalk seating, remains the most characterful. The Clayton location serves the business community; the Tower Grove South location has a neighborhood regulars vibe.

Kaldi's roasts its own beans and takes its sourcing seriously. The lattes are consistently excellent, and the food program — pastries, sandwiches — is better than average for a coffee-focused shop. For St. Louis newcomers, Kaldi's is often the first recommendation for reliable quality in a comfortable setting.

University City (The Loop)

Meshuggah Cafe

Meshuggah has been a fixture on The Loop — the vibrant stretch of Delmar Boulevard in University City — since 1994. It occupies a corner space with mismatched furniture, walls covered in concert posters and local art, and a playlist that leans toward independent music. Open late on weekends (sometimes until midnight), Meshuggah serves the students and young professionals who give The Loop its energy.

The coffee is solid rather than exceptional — this is not a precision espresso destination. What Meshuggah offers is atmosphere and accessibility: a place to sit for three hours with a book, a friend, or a laptop without feeling rushed. The chai lattes and blended drinks are particularly popular.

Cherokee Street

Comet Coffee

Comet Coffee occupies a vintage-influenced space on Cherokee Street, one of St. Louis's most creatively diverse commercial corridors. The shop's aesthetic — retro signage, warm wood, curated vintage objects — matches the neighborhood's character. The espresso drinks are well-made; the drip coffee program uses quality single-origin beans. Comet also serves as a neighborhood gathering point, which Cherokee Street needs given its role as an arts and culture hub.

The shop's proximity to Cherokee Street's antique dealers, galleries, and taquerias makes it a natural base for an afternoon of exploring the street on foot.

Dutchtown / South City

Foundation Grounds

Foundation Grounds is a community-minded coffee shop in the Dutchtown neighborhood — one of St. Louis's most historically working-class and currently up-and-coming south-side communities. The shop sources its beans from a social enterprise roaster and maintains a commitment to the neighborhood that goes beyond mere presence. The coffee is good; the atmosphere is genuinely welcoming in a way that some hipper shops are not.

Foundation Grounds also hosts community events, open mic nights, and neighborhood meetings, making it a genuine third place in the sociological sense.

Maplewood

Water Street Coffee

Maplewood is a small city immediately west of St. Louis proper, with a commercial district on Manchester Avenue that has become one of the best dining and coffee destinations in the metro area. Water Street Coffee is a standout on that strip — a pour-over-focused shop with a meticulous approach to extraction and a comfortable, unhurried atmosphere. The single-origin rotating menu is well-chosen; the baristas can explain the coffee's provenance without being pretentious about it.

Downtown / Washington Avenue

Coma Coffee Roasters

Coma occupies a large industrial-chic space on Washington Avenue in downtown St. Louis. It serves as the coffee destination for the downtown arts district and loft community, and it pulls this off with a strong espresso program and a menu that goes beyond coffee into natural wines and craft beers. The space is large and works as a destination at multiple times of day. The roasting program uses interesting direct-trade sourcing that distinguishes Coma from shops that simply white-label third-party beans.

Notable Mentions: The Expanding Scene

St. Louis's coffee scene continues to grow. Several additional shops worth tracking:

  • Stringbean Coffee (South City) — excellent pour-over with a rotating seasonal menu
  • Northwest Coffee Roasting Company (North City) — roastery cafe on Natural Bridge, pioneering the north side coffee scene
  • Half & Half (Clayton) — breakfast-focused cafe known for exceptional brunch as much as coffee
  • The Coffee Bar (Midtown) — attached to a coworking space; great for extended work sessions

Coffee and Neighborhood Exploration

The best St. Louis coffee experience is usually built around a neighborhood walk. Use Kaldi's DeMun as the base for exploring Clayton's architecture and restaurants. Start at Sump and walk through Benton Park's Victorian brick blocks. Use Comet Coffee as the anchor for a Cherokee Street afternoon. Blueprint in The Grove pairs naturally with a walk through Forest Park (just blocks away) or dinner on Manchester Avenue in the evening.
